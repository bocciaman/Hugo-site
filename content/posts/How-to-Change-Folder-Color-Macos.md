In this tutorial I'll show you how to create custom-colored folders in macOS Monterey. If you've ever looked at a Finder window full of identical blue folders and wished you could tell them apart at a glance, this one's for you. Fair warning up front, though: as of right now there's no way to automate this — it's a hands-on, multi-step process. I'll walk you through every step so it's painless, and I'll explain why a little color goes a long way for staying organized.

## Why Bother Changing Folder Colors?

Before we get into the how, let me make the case for the why. By default every folder on your Mac is the same shade of blue, which means your eyes have to read each label to find what you're looking for. Color changes that. If your most important project folder is red, your archive is gray, and your "in progress" work is green, you can spot the right one instantly without reading a single word. It's a small visual cue, but for anyone who keeps a lot of folders on their desktop or in a busy Finder window, it genuinely speeds things up and cuts down on clutter-fatigue. Think of it as color-coding your physical desk, except it's your digital one.

## Step One: Copy the Folder's Icon

Here's the whole process, and it's easier than it sounds once you've done it once. Start by selecting the folder you want to customize, right-click on it, and choose **Get Info** (Command + I does the same thing). In the Get Info window that opens, look at the small folder icon in the top-left corner and click it once so it's highlighted. Then go up to the menu bar, choose **Edit**, and select **Copy**. You've just copied the folder's current icon image to your clipboard — that's the thing we're about to recolor.

## Step Two: Recolor It in Preview

Now open **Preview**, the image app that ships with every Mac. From Preview's **File** menu, choose **New from Clipboard**. This takes the folder icon you just copied and turns it into an editable image you can play with.

With that image open, go to the **Tools** menu and choose **Adjust Color**. You'll get a panel of sliders — hue, saturation, brightness, and more. The **hue** slider is the star of the show here; dragging it shifts the blue folder toward whatever color you want, whether that's a warm red, a deep purple, or a soft green. Tweak saturation and brightness to fine-tune how bold or muted the color looks. Take a moment to land on something you actually like, then save the image to your desktop so you have it ready for the next step.

## Step Three: Paste Your New Icon Back

Almost there. Go back to the **Get Info** window for your folder and click that little folder icon in the top-left corner again to select it. This time choose **Edit** from the menu bar and select **Paste**. Your custom-colored icon replaces the default blue one, and just like that, your folder stands out from the crowd. Repeat the process for any other folders you want to color, and you can build a whole system that makes sense to you at a glance.

## A Few Tips

A couple of things I've learned doing this. First, pick a small, consistent palette rather than coloring everything — if every folder is a different bright color, you lose the benefit, because nothing stands out anymore. Use color intentionally: a few key folders, not all of them. Second, if you ever want to go back to the default, just open Get Info, select the icon, and press Delete — the folder reverts to standard blue. And third, you don't *have* to recolor the original blue icon; you could paste in any image you like using the same Get Info trick, which is a fun way to give special folders a totally custom look.

## Building a Color System That Actually Helps

A little planning turns this from a fun cosmetic trick into a genuinely useful organizing system. The key is to assign *meaning* to your colors rather than coloring folders at random. For example, you might use red for anything urgent or high-priority, green for active projects you're currently working on, gray for archived or finished material, and blue (the default) for everything else that's just routine. The moment you open a Finder window, your eyes go straight to the red folder because you've trained yourself that red means "deal with this."

You can also color by category instead of priority — one color for work, another for personal, another for finances — whatever framework matches how your brain sorts things. The point is consistency: once a color means something specific to you, it becomes a navigation aid instead of just decoration. And remember you're not limited to recoloring the standard folder shape. Using the exact same Get Info trick, you can paste in *any* image as a folder's icon, so a special project folder could get a custom logo or a photo that makes it instantly recognizable. Start simple with a handful of meaningful colors, and expand your system as you figure out what's actually helpful.

## Conclusion

And that's the whole workaround. It's more steps than it should be, but once you've run through it a couple of times it becomes second nature, and the payoff — a Finder you can navigate by color instead of by reading every label — is well worth the few minutes.

I'll be honest, though: there really *should* be a built-in, one-click way to do this, and ideally a way to automate it too. As of now there isn't, and if I ever find a method to script this properly, you'll be the first to know. So consider this an open request to Apple — give us native folder colors already. Until then, this trick gets the job done.
