## Why You'd Want Safari to Start Fresh

Let me make the case first, because not everyone realizes this is even adjustable. By default, Safari can reopen every tab you had open when you last quit. For some people that's convenient — you pick up right where you left off. But for a lot of us, it's the opposite of helpful. You quit Safari to *clear your head*, and then it greets you with the same fifteen tabs you were trying to escape. Those tabs also quietly eat memory and clutter your window the moment you launch. If you prefer to start each session with a clean slate — a single fresh window or your start page — this one setting change fixes it for good.

## The Step-by-Step Fix in Sonoma

The good news is this is genuinely a one-setting change; it just needs to be the *right* setting in the right place. Here's the path in macOS Sonoma:

1. Open **Safari**, then from the menu bar go to **Safari Settings** (you can also press `Command + ,`).
2. Click the **General** tab.
3. Find the first dropdown menu — labeled **"Safari opens with"** — and select **"A new window."**

That's it. With "A new window" chosen, Safari will no longer drag your previous session's tabs back every time it launches. Instead you'll get a clean new window, exactly the fresh start you're after.

## Understanding the Related Options

While you're in there, it's worth knowing the neighboring settings so you can dial in exactly the behavior you want. The "Safari opens with" dropdown also offers options like "A new private window" and "All windows from last session" — that last one is the culprit if your tabs keep coming back, so make sure you've moved *away* from it. Just below, the **"New windows open with"** and **"New tabs open with"** menus let you decide what actually shows up in that fresh window: your homepage, a start page, an empty page, or your favorites. Pairing "A new window" with a clean start page gives you the tidiest possible launch.

## What If You Still Want Some Tabs Back?

It's worth saying that "start fresh" doesn't have to mean "lose everything." If you like a clean launch but occasionally wish you could recover the tabs you just closed, Safari has you covered separately from this setting. The **History → Reopen All Windows from Last Session** menu item brings back your previous session on demand, and **Command + Z** right after closing a tab reopens it. So you get the best of both worlds: a tidy, fast start every time, with the safety net of recovering your old tabs in the rare case you actually need them. That's the combination I'd recommend — default to a clean slate, and lean on Safari's recovery options when something important slips away.

A related tip: if it's really just a handful of sites you always want open, don't rely on session restore for them at all. Instead, save them as a **bookmark folder** and open them together in one click, or set them as your Safari favorites. That gives you intentional, reliable access to your "always open" sites without dragging back the random clutter of an entire previous session.

## Why This Changed in Sonoma

If you followed my older video and couldn't find the same options, you're not imagining it — Apple periodically reorganizes Safari's settings, and Sonoma shuffled a few things around compared to earlier macOS versions. That's the whole reason for this update: the *goal* is the same as before, but the exact menu layout is current as of Sonoma. Whenever Apple ships a big macOS update, it's worth double-checking settings like this, since a tutorial from a couple of versions back can point you at a menu that's since moved.

## Conclusion

Stopping Safari from reopening your previous tabs is a small tweak with a real quality-of-life payoff. Head to Safari Settings, open the General tab, and set "Safari opens with" to "A new window," and you'll be greeted by a clean slate every single time you launch the browser. It keeps your sessions tidy, trims unnecessary memory use, and lets you start each browsing session on your terms rather than buried under yesterday's tabs. A tiny change, but one you'll appreciate every day.
