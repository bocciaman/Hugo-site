## How the Book Reading Log Works

Let me go a little deeper on the first shortcut, because the "append to an existing note" behavior is the clever part. When you run the Book Reading Log, it prompts you for the book details — title, author, and the pages you've read — and then it checks whether a note for that book already exists. If it doesn't, it creates a fresh, organized note. If it does, it *appends* your new entry to the bottom of that same note instead of making a duplicate. The result is one tidy, running log per book that grows as you read, all living in Apple Notes where it syncs across your devices automatically. No separate reading app, no lost history, no subscription — just a clean record you actually own.

## How the Weight Loss Diary Works

The Weight Loss Diary shortcut is built around removing every excuse not to log. It asks for your weight in pounds, converts it to kilograms on the fly, writes the reading directly into **Apple Health** (so it joins all your other health data and shows up in trends and charts), and also saves a dated entry in Notes for an at-a-glance history. The pound-to-kilogram conversion is a nice example of how Shortcuts can do small bits of logic for you — you enter the number in the unit you think in, and the shortcut handles the math and stores it consistently. One tap from the Action Button and you're done; there's no app to open and no account to sign into.

## The Action Button Advantage

Both of these are tied to my iPhone's **Action Button**, and that's the secret sauce that makes them stick. The biggest reason people abandon tracking habits is friction — if logging takes more than a couple of seconds, you eventually stop doing it. By mapping a shortcut to the Action Button (or to a Home Screen icon or a Back Tap, if your iPhone doesn't have an Action Button), you reduce logging to a single press. That tiny bit of convenience is often the difference between a habit that lasts and one that fizzles out after a week.

## Why Use Shortcuts Instead of Apps?

Most tracking apps want a subscription, your data, or both. iPhone Shortcuts, by contrast, run locally on your device, sync through your own iCloud account, and integrate with the apps already on your phone — Notes, Health, Reminders, and more. They're free, they're fast, and they're fully customizable to how *you* actually live, rather than how some app developer assumed you do. If you want a tracker to behave differently — log a different unit, store data somewhere else, ask an extra question — you can just open the shortcut and change it. That kind of control is something a closed commercial app almost never gives you, and it's why I keep replacing apps with simple, purpose-built shortcuts.

## Make Them Your Own

One last thing: because these are shortcuts and not locked-down apps, you can reshape them to fit your life. Swap pounds for kilograms if that's how you weigh in, change where the notes are saved, add a prompt for how you're feeling that day, or have the reading log also track a page-count goal. That flexibility is the whole point — you're not bending your habits to fit someone else's app, you're building a tracker that works exactly the way you do.
