In this post I walk you through the pros and cons of two different iOS clipboard managers. One is a paid app called Clippo that you can buy from the App Store. The other is a free clipboard manager shortcut I picked up from fellow YouTuber Christopher Lawley. Both aim to solve the same problem — iOS only remembers the *last* thing you copied — but they go about it in very different ways, and after living with both, I've got a clear preference. Let me explain why.

## The Problem Both Apps Are Trying to Solve

First, a quick word on why you'd even want a clipboard manager. By default, iOS only holds one item on the clipboard at a time. Copy something new and whatever you copied before is gone for good. On the Mac we've had clipboard history tools for years, but on iPhone and iPad you have to add that capability yourself. A clipboard manager keeps a running list of everything you've copied, so you can scroll back and paste something from earlier instead of losing it. Once you're used to having that safety net, going back to a single-item clipboard feels genuinely limiting.

## Clippo: The Paid Option

[Clippo](https://apps.apple.com/us/app/clippo-clipboard-manager/id1092865336) is the paid clipboard manager available on the App Store. Here's how it works in practice: you copy the text you want to keep, then you open Clippo, and it asks whether you'd like to save that text. Say yes, and it gets added to an internal list inside the app that you can return to later.

That flow is where my first reservation comes in. Because iOS limits what apps can do with the clipboard in the background, Clippo can't just silently capture everything you copy — you have to actively open the app and confirm each save. It works, but it's a few extra taps every time, and it means you have to *remember* to save something before you copy your next thing, which somewhat defeats the "never lose a copy" promise.

On the plus side, Clippo does offer some **advanced settings**, including a feature to send copied text over to your Mac. That sounds great on paper, and when it works it's handy. In my experience, though, that cross-device feature didn't work consistently — sometimes it went through, sometimes it just didn't — which made it hard to rely on for anything important.

## The Clipboard Manager Shortcut: The Free Option

The other contender is a free clipboard manager built as an Apple Shortcut, which I got from Christopher Lawley's channel. I lean toward this one even though it has a dependency: it relies on the [Data Jar](https://apps.apple.com/us/app/data-jar/id1453273600) app to actually store the copied content. Data Jar is free too, so you're still not paying anything, but it is a second piece you need installed for the shortcut to work.

The shortcut primarily does its thing through notifications, which fits nicely into the iOS way of doing things — you interact with it via the Shortcuts app and notification prompts rather than opening a separate full app. I'll be transparent, though: in my video, the shortcut actually didn't fire correctly when I went to demo it. Just my luck, right? That's live tech for you. Even with that hiccup, the overall approach — free, built on native Shortcuts, no purchase required — is the one I find myself drawn to.

## So Which One Wins?

Both tools get you multi-item clipboard history on iOS, and honestly either one is better than the stock single-item clipboard. Clippo is a polished, self-contained app with some extra features, but it's paid and its standout cross-device trick was unreliable for me. The shortcut is free and built on Apple's own Shortcuts framework, at the cost of leaning on Data Jar and being a little less plug-and-play.

If your priority is a tidy, all-in-one app and you don't mind paying, Clippo is a reasonable pick. If, like me, you'd rather keep things free and native, the shortcut route is the way to go even with its quirks.

## Conclusion

At the end of the day, my preference comes down to a simple philosophy I keep coming back to: I'll almost always choose a free, native solution over a paid, third-party one. The clipboard manager shortcut isn't perfect, and it stumbled in my demo, but it's free, it's built on the tools Apple already gives us, and it does the job. That's the kind of automation I like to champion. Give both a try if you're curious — your own workflow might tip the scales differently — but for me, free and native wins.
